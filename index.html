1 Customer Psychology

Understand what homeowners are thinking before consultants ever speak. Every interaction is shaped by the emotional journey that started long before you arrived.

The Most Important Principle

Homeowners almost never call Baird because they want foundation repair. They call because certainty has disappeared.

By the time someone schedules an inspection, they've often spent days or weeks thinking:

  • "Maybe it's nothing..."
  • "Maybe it's getting worse..."
  • "What if my house is unsafe?"
  • "How much is this going to cost?"
  • "Am I about to get taken advantage of?"

The consultant's job is to reduce uncertainty—not increase pressure.

The Emotional Timeline

Every homeowner moves through predictable emotional states. Understanding these stages allows the consultant to lead instead of react.

1

Stage 1 – Problem Recognition

Internal dialogue: "Hopefully this isn't serious."
▾
Typical Triggers
  • New drywall crack
  • Existing crack growing
  • Doors sticking
  • Windows won't open
  • Sloping floor
  • Water intrusion
  • Neighbor had foundation work
  • Home inspection report
What's Happening

At this point the homeowner is usually hoping they're wrong. They're not looking for a repair—they're looking for reassurance that it's nothing.

2

Stage 2 – Internet Research

Within minutes they're overwhelmed
▾
What They Find
  • Horror stories
  • Contractor scams
  • Conflicting advice
  • YouTube engineers
  • Reddit opinions
  • Articles telling them their home is failing
What Happens

Their anxiety increases. They search for "foundation crack," "doors sticking," "foundation repair near me," and "cost of foundation repair." Within minutes they're overwhelmed with conflicting information and fear-based content.

3

Stage 3 – Scheduling

Mixed emotions: excitement, fear, distrust
▾
Emotional State
  • Excitement: "I'll finally know."
  • Fear: "I don't want to hear bad news."
  • Distrust: "I know he's coming to sell me."
What This Means

The appointment itself creates mixed emotions. The customer is simultaneously hoping for answers and dreading them. They're also pre-loaded with distrust because they assume every consultant is there to sell them something.

Key Takeaway

Homeowners don't call because they want repair. They call because certainty disappeared. Your job is to restore it.

Arrival Psychology

When the truck pulls up the homeowner immediately begins evaluating. Questions they're asking subconsciously:

  • Do they look professional?
  • Are they rushed?
  • Are they respectful?
  • Do they seem trustworthy?
  • Can they explain things?
  • Will they pressure me?

The sale begins before anyone speaks.

The First Five Minutes

The customer isn't evaluating technical ability. They're evaluating emotional safety. They're deciding: Can I relax around this person?

Top consultants lower defenses immediately through:

  • Professional appearance
  • Confident posture
  • Relaxed pace
  • Eye contact
  • Warm introduction
  • Clear expectations
  • Respect for the home

During the Inspection

Most homeowners don't know what the consultant is looking for. That creates a dangerous vacuum.

If the consultant disappears for 45 minutes, the homeowner imagines: "They found something terrible" OR "They're just trying to make this seem complicated."

Instead: Narrate the inspection.

Example: "I'm checking whether these cracks line up with the elevation changes outside."

Now the homeowner feels included.

Information vs. Interpretation

Customers observe symptoms. Consultants interpret them.

  • Customer sees: Crack. Consultant sees: Settlement pattern.
  • Customer sees: Door sticking. Consultant sees: Differential movement.

Never assume customers know the connection. Explain it.

Fear of Being Sold

Every homeowner carries this concern. Questions in their mind:

  • Will they exaggerate?
  • Will they invent problems?
  • Can I trust the diagnosis?

This is why education always precedes recommendation.

The Trust Equation

Trust grows from four variables:

  • Credibility — Technical competence
  • Reliability — Doing exactly what was promised
  • Transparency — Showing, not hiding, evidence
  • Empathy — Acknowledging homeowner concerns

If one variable is missing, trust weakens.

The Moment They Believe You

This almost never happens during pricing. It happens when the homeowner says something like:

  • "That actually makes sense."
  • "I never thought about it that way."

Understanding creates confidence. Confidence precedes buying.

Decision Anxiety

Even after believing the diagnosis, new fears emerge:

  • Can I afford this?
  • What if another company says something different?
  • Should I wait?
  • Will this really fix it?

This is where weaker consultants begin "hard selling." Top consultants continue educating.

Why People Delay

Most delays have nothing to do with money. They are emotional:

  • Fear of making a mistake
  • Need spouse approval
  • Need certainty
  • Need comparison
  • Need processing time

Teach consultants to diagnose hesitation—not attack it.

Financing Psychology

Many customers assume financing equals failure. Instead of discussing payment, discuss affordability.

Instead of: "It's $18,000."

Frame it: "This is an investment in protecting a several-hundred-thousand-dollar asset."

After Signing

Customers often experience buyer's remorse. Internal thoughts: "Did I overpay?" "Should I have gotten another quote?"

This is where reassurance matters. A follow-up call within 24 hours dramatically reinforces confidence.

Installation Day Psychology

Customers now evaluate whether the company matches the sales experience. Communication becomes more important than speed. The installer reinforces trust.

Post-Installation

Now customers become advocates. Their mindset shifts from "I hope I made the right decision" to "I'm glad that's behind us."

This is the best time for:

  • Reviews
  • Testimonials
  • Referrals

Psychological Profiles

E

The Engineer

Needs evidence, measurements, photos, data
▾
How To Engage

Don't oversimplify. This customer wants to see the numbers, the photos, the elevation readings. Show your work. Walk them through the data. They'll trust you when they can verify your findings themselves.

S

The Skeptic

Needs transparency, patience, no pressure
▾
How To Engage

Expect many questions. Answer every one without defensiveness. This customer has likely been burned before or doesn't trust contractors. Patience and transparency are your greatest tools. Never pressure.

B

The Emotional Buyer

Needs safety, reassurance, confidence
▾
How To Engage

Less technical explanation. More reassurance. This customer needs to feel safe. Focus on the outcome—a stable, secure home—not the engineering details. Build confidence through empathy.

F

The Financial Planner

Needs ROI, home value, warranty, payment options
▾
How To Engage

This customer thinks in terms of investment. Frame the repair as protecting home value. Discuss warranty as a long-term asset. Present payment options clearly. They want to know the numbers make sense.

P

The Busy Professional

Needs efficiency, clear recommendations, minimal complexity
▾
How To Engage

Be efficient. Get to the point. This customer values their time above all else. Give them a clear recommendation with clear next steps. Don't waste time on details they didn't ask for.

D

The DIY Researcher

Has watched dozens of YouTube videos
▾
How To Engage

Treat respectfully. Never argue. Invite discussion. This customer has done their homework—some of it good, some of it bad. Acknowledge what they've learned and bridge it to your expertise.

What Great Consultants Actually Sell

They sell:

  • Confidence
  • Safety
  • Understanding
  • Predictability
  • Professionalism

Not concrete. Not steel. Not piers.

Supervisor Coaching Questions

Instead of asking: "Why didn't they buy?"

Ask:

  • "When did they begin trusting you?"
  • "What evidence convinced them?"
  • "What emotional concern remained unresolved?"
  • "Did they understand the problem before you recommended a solution?"

Those questions reveal where the sales process actually broke down.

Key Takeaway

Great consultants sell confidence, safety, and understanding—not concrete, steel, or piers.

2 Discovery Framework

Diagnose people before diagnosing homes. The inspection identifies structural problems. Discovery identifies buying motivations. Both are equally important.

The Rule of Discovery

Never recommend before understanding:

  • Problem — What's happening structurally
  • Impact — How it affects the homeowner
  • Priority — Where this falls among their concerns
  • Decision Process — Who needs to be involved
  • Budget Comfort — What they expect and can invest
  • Timeline — When they need this resolved

Two customers with identical foundations may buy for completely different reasons.

The Discovery Funnel

Every appointment follows this sequence:

  • Rapport → Current Concern → History → Impact → Future Goals → Decision Process → Financial Comfort → Commitment
1

Stage 1 — Opening Discovery

Understand why today
▾
Questions To Ask
  • "What made you decide to schedule the inspection now?"
  • "When did you first notice the issue?"
  • "What changed recently?"
  • "What concerns you the most?"

These establish urgency.

2

Stage 2 — History

Learn the property's story
▾
Questions To Ask
  • "Have you noticed this before?"
  • "Has anyone inspected the home previously?"
  • "Have repairs ever been made?"
  • "Have you owned the home long?"
  • "Did the previous owner mention anything?"
3

Stage 3 — Symptoms

Avoid yes/no questions
▾
Questions To Ask
  • "What have you noticed inside?"
  • "What have you noticed outside?"
  • "What rooms concern you?"
  • "What changes have you seen over time?"
4

Stage 4 — Impact

Connect symptoms to emotion
▾
Questions To Ask
  • "How has this affected you?"
  • "What worries you most?"
  • "Are you concerned about resale?"
  • "Has this changed your renovation plans?"
  • "What happens if nothing changes?"

Now the consultant understands consequences.

5

Stage 5 — Future

Different future plans require different presentations
▾
Questions To Ask
  • "How long do you plan on living here?"
  • "Any remodeling planned?"
  • "Planning to sell?"
  • "Do you expect to pass the home to family?"
6

Stage 6 — Decision Process

Never assume. Ask.
▾
Questions To Ask
  • "Who will be involved in making this decision?"
  • "Would you like everyone present during the review?"
  • "Have you already spoken with other companies?"
  • "Are you planning on comparing multiple estimates?"

Knowing this early prevents surprises later.

7

Stage 7 — Financial Comfort

Reveal expectations without creating defensiveness
▾
Never Ask

"What's your budget?"

Instead Ask
  • "Have you done any research into what foundation repairs typically cost?"
  • "What were you expecting this project might involve?"
  • "If repairs are needed, would financing be something you'd want to explore, or do you already have a plan in mind?"
8

Stage 8 — Commitment Questions

Uncover remaining barriers while there's still time
▾
Near The End Of The Inspection
  • "If we identify a solution that gives you confidence, what would you like the next step to be?"
  • "If we can answer all your questions today, is there anything else that would keep you from moving forward?"

These aren't "trial closes." They uncover remaining barriers while there's still time to address them.

Reading Buying Signals

S

Strong Buying Signals

The customer is ready to move forward
▾
Recognize These
  • The homeowner starts asking about installation timing
  • They ask how long repairs will take
  • They ask about warranties or transferable coverage
  • They begin discussing how to prepare the home
  • They ask whether furniture needs to be moved
  • They ask detailed financing questions rather than general price questions
  • They involve a spouse in practical planning rather than in skepticism
W

Weak Buying Signals

Caution: these indicate hesitation
▾
Watch For These
  • "We'll think about it."
  • "We're just gathering information."
  • Repeatedly steering the conversation back to price before understanding the problem
  • Looking at a phone or becoming disengaged during the explanation
  • Introducing new decision-makers late in the process

Discovery Scorecard for Ride-Alongs

One of the highest-value coaching tools. During every ride-along, evaluate whether the consultant:

  • Set expectations — Explained appointment agenda and timeline
  • Built rapport — Established trust without wasting time
  • Identified trigger event — Learned why the homeowner called today
  • Explored symptoms — Asked open-ended questions before inspecting
  • Uncovered emotional impact — Connected technical issues to homeowner concerns
  • Clarified goals — Learned future plans for the property
  • Identified decision-makers — Confirmed who needs to be involved
  • Discussed financial readiness — Explored expectations without pressuring
  • Active listening — Asked follow-up questions and summarized accurately
  • Transition to inspection — Moved naturally from conversation to technical evaluation

The benchmark isn't "Did they ask every question?" It's "Did they understand the homeowner well enough to deliver a recommendation that felt personal rather than generic?"

Key Takeaway

Discovery is the appointment. Everything after it is confirmation. Never recommend before you understand the person, not just the problem.

3 Objection Handling

Diagnose objections instead of memorizing scripts. An objection is simply an unanswered question. The homeowner is rarely saying "No"—they're saying "I don't have enough confidence yet."

The Philosophy of Objections

The average salesperson views objections as resistance. Top consultants understand: an objection is simply an unanswered question.

Every objection falls into one (or more) of five categories:

  • Financial — Confidence they can afford it
  • Technical — Confidence the diagnosis is correct
  • Trust — Confidence in Baird
  • Timing — Confidence action is necessary now
  • Decision — Confidence they're making the right choice

The solution changes depending on which confidence gap exists.

Universal Objection Framework

Teach every consultant the same sequence:

  • Step 1 — Don't defend. Slow down. Curiosity beats persuasion.
  • Step 2 — Acknowledge. "I completely understand." "I'd probably ask the same thing."
  • Step 3 — Clarify. "What specifically concerns you?" Never assume.
  • Step 4 — Diagnose. Is this money? Trust? Fear? Need for control? Need for spouse?
  • Step 5 — Educate. Never argue. Never debate. Help them understand.
  • Step 6 — Confirm. "Does that answer your concern?" Never continue if the objection remains unresolved.
C

"It's too expensive."

Financial · Almost never about the actual number
▾
Reality

Almost never about the number itself. Usually means: "I wasn't expecting that," "I don't understand the value," or "I'm afraid of making a mistake."

Response Framework

"I understand. Most homeowners haven't purchased foundation repair before, so seeing the investment can definitely be surprising."

Pause.

"Can I ask—was it higher than you expected, or is it more about deciding whether the repair is worth doing?"

If They Expected Lower

Educate. Foundation repair involves engineering, steel, concrete, heavy equipment, permits, and lifetime solutions. Help them understand what creates value.

Coaching Note

Never immediately offer discounts. Discounting before value destroys credibility.

T

"I need to think about it."

Decision · Unresolved certainty
▾
Meaning

"I haven't reached certainty." Not "I need silence."

Questions
  • "What would you like to think through?"
  • "What questions remain?"
  • "What information would help?"
  • "What hasn't been answered yet?"
Supervisor Diagnosis

If this objection occurs frequently, the consultant likely: presented too early, didn't discover enough, or didn't educate sufficiently.

O

"I want another opinion."

Trust · Confidence through comparison
▾
Better Approach

Never fight this. Agree: "I completely understand."

Then ask: "What would you want another company to help you confirm?"

Excellent consultants aren't threatened. If the customer says "I just want to make sure," respond: "I think that's reasonable. If another qualified company tells you the same thing we've discussed today, would that give you confidence moving forward?"

Coaching Principle

Confident consultants welcome comparisons. Weak consultants become defensive.

S

"I want to shop around."

Comparison · Different from seeking another opinion
▾
What's Happening

The homeowner has already decided repairs may be necessary. They're comparing providers. Now emphasize: inspection quality, warranty, experience, installation standards, communication, longevity. Not price.

Ask

"What will you be comparing?"

If they answer Price—educate. If they answer Trust—you're winning.

M

"We need to talk about it."

Decision · Missing stakeholder
▾
Clarify

"Absolutely. Who else would you like involved?"

Common issues: spouse absent, adult children, financial advisor, investor partner.

Coaching Principle

Great consultants identify decision-makers within the first fifteen minutes—not at the kitchen table after presenting.

F

"We can't afford it."

Financial · Requires genuine empathy
▾
Approach

Never minimize. Instead: "I understand. Protecting a home is important, but it also has to fit your family's financial situation."

Then Explore

"Would it be helpful if we looked at financing options together?"

Notice: you're offering options. Not pressure.

P

"Your competitor is cheaper."

Comparison · Scope vs. price
▾
Ask

"What differences did you notice between the recommendations?"

Sometimes they aren't comparing equal scopes. Different pier counts, different warranty, different drainage recommendations, different engineering approach. Help them compare solutions. Not invoices.

W

"We'll wait."

Timing · Urgency unclear
▾
Explore

"What would you like to wait for?"

Possible answers: savings, spouse, selling, weather, tax refund, comfort. Each has a different conversation.

D

"I don't think it's that bad."

Skepticism · Evidence gap
▾
Never Argue

Walk back through evidence: photos, measurements, elevation readings, symptoms. Explain progression. Not catastrophe. The consultant becomes a guide, not a salesperson.

Sales Supervisor Coaching Questions

Instead of: "What should you have said?"

Ask:

  • "What confidence was missing?"
  • "When did the conversation change?"
  • "What evidence did you present?"
  • "What question should you have asked instead?"

Those questions develop judgment rather than memorization.

Key Takeaway

Every objection is an unanswered question. Diagnose which confidence is missing before you respond.

4 Performance Improvement

Self-diagnose and improve without waiting for management. Poor sales performance is almost never caused by laziness—it's usually caused by one of four issues.

The Supervisor's Mission

Poor performance is usually caused by one of four issues:

  • Skill deficit
  • Process inconsistency
  • Confidence deficit
  • Activity deficiency

The supervisor's job is to diagnose the root cause—not just address the symptom.

The Performance Formula

Knowledge + Skill + Confidence + Consistency + Activity = Performance

If one element weakens, overall performance declines.

Performance Diagnostic Flow

Coach the earliest point of failure:

  • Low Close Rate → Enough appointments? No → Activity Coaching
  • Yes → Inspection quality strong? No → Technical Training
  • Yes → Discovery complete? No → Questioning Coaching
  • Yes → Presentation effective? No → Communication Coaching
  • Yes → Objections managed? No → Objection Practice
  • Yes → Closing confidence? No → Closing Coaching
  • Yes → External factors

Fixing downstream behaviors rarely solves upstream problems.

Common Performance Gaps

1

Low Trust Builder

Symptoms: guarded homeowner, few questions, price dominant, short conversations
▾
Likely Cause

Poor rapport and expectation setting.

Coaching
  • Practice introductions
  • Slow the pace
  • Set a clear agenda
  • Demonstrate respect for the homeowner and their property
  • Build confidence before discussing technical findings
2

Weak Discovery

Symptoms: generic presentations, frequent "need to think," disengaged customer
▾
Likely Cause

The consultant talked more than they asked.

Coaching
  • Increase open-ended questions
  • Practice active listening
  • Require consultants to summarize the homeowner's concerns before presenting
  • Evaluate discovery using the ride-along scorecard
3

Technical Knowledge Gap

Symptoms: difficulty explaining settlement, reliance on jargon, customer confusion
▾
Coaching
  • Weekly product and engineering refreshers
  • Practice explaining foundation concepts in plain language
  • Use role-play where the "customer" has no construction background
4

Poor Inspection Discipline

Symptoms: missed symptoms, inconsistent recommendations, weak documentation
▾
Coaching
  • Standardize a room-by-room inspection sequence
  • Use photo checklists
  • Conduct field audits with immediate feedback
  • Require consultants to explain what they're inspecting as they go
5

Presentation Overload

Symptoms: customer overwhelmed, long monologues, eyes glaze over
▾
Coaching

Teach "Chunk and Check." Present one concept. Pause. Ask: "Does that make sense?" Only continue after confirming understanding.

6

Fear of Asking for the Business

Symptoms: presentation ends awkwardly, no clear next step
▾
Coaching

Build confidence in natural transitions: "Based on everything we've reviewed, here's what I recommend..." Practice this until it feels conversational rather than scripted.

7

Price-Centric Selling

Symptoms: early discounting, frequent price objections, margin erosion
▾
Coaching

Delay pricing until after: Discovery → Inspection → Education → Recommendation. Value first. Price second.

The Coaching Cycle

Every coaching interaction should follow the same cadence:

  • Observe – Watch the appointment without interrupting unless necessary
  • Reflect – Ask the consultant to self-assess before giving feedback
  • Diagnose – Identify the earliest breakdown in the sales process
  • Coach – Focus on one or two behaviors, not ten
  • Practice – Role-play the corrected behavior immediately
  • Reinforce – Follow up on the next ride-along to confirm improvement

30-Day Performance Improvement Plan

  • Week 1: Observation and baseline metrics. Identify root causes and establish goals.
  • Week 2: Discovery and rapport coaching. Improved homeowner engagement.
  • Week 3: Presentation and objection handling. Higher confidence and fewer unresolved objections.
  • Week 4: Closing execution and reinforcement. Increased close rate and process consistency.

Ride-Along Debrief Framework

Every field coaching session should end with these five questions:

  • What went well today that you'd repeat?
  • Where did you feel the customer's confidence increase?
  • Where did the conversation become more difficult?
  • If you could restart one five-minute segment of the appointment, which would it be and why?
  • What's one behavior you'll intentionally improve before your next appointment?

This format develops self-awareness first, allowing the supervisor's coaching to reinforce rather than replace critical thinking.

Core Leadership Principle

The strongest Sales Supervisors don't create consultants who memorize scripts—they create consultants who can diagnose homeowners, adapt to different personalities, communicate complex structural issues simply, and execute a repeatable sales process under a wide range of circumstances. Their coaching focuses on behaviors that can be repeated and measured, producing consistent customer experiences and predictable sales performance across the entire team.

Key Takeaway

Performance problems are almost never about laziness. Diagnose the root cause: skill, process, confidence, or activity.

⚡ If This Then That

Pattern recognition is the foundation of professional judgment. Every scenario follows the same structure so you can instantly recognize what's happening and what to do about it.

1

"I need to think about it."

Psychology · Objections · Decision Confidence
▾
IF THIS…
"I need to think about it."

They're signaling unresolved uncertainty—not requesting silence.

THEN THAT…

The customer probably lacks confidence, not information. They may still have unanswered questions, fear of making a mistake, need for another decision maker, unresolved trust, or decision anxiety.

Great Reps Notice…
  • The objection appeared before price was discussed
  • The customer stopped asking questions
  • Eye contact decreased
  • They became quieter
  • They never acknowledged understanding
Common Mistakes
  • Immediately lowering price
  • Talking faster to fill silence
  • Repeating the presentation
  • Becoming defensive
  • Assuming the objection is money
  • Asking "So what will it take?"
Better Approach

Slow down. Ask: "What would you like to think through?" Listen. Clarify. Identify the real concern. Then educate.

Why This Works

Customers rarely buy because they are pressured. They buy because uncertainty has been reduced.

Key Principle

Confidence is created through understanding, not persuasion.

2

"We're getting another estimate."

Objections · Trust · Comparison
▾
IF THIS…
"We're getting a couple more estimates."

The customer isn't rejecting you. They're seeking confidence through comparison.

THEN THAT…

They want confidence through comparison—not necessarily a lower price. They may lack a frame of reference for cost, have been burned before, need external validation, or have an absent spouse.

Great Reps Notice…
  • They're still engaged in the conversation
  • They ask thoughtful comparison questions
  • They continue discussing installation and warranty
  • They're seeking reassurance more than negotiation
Common Mistakes
  • Speaking negatively about competitors
  • Immediately offering discounts
  • Becoming defensive
  • Pressuring for a same-day decision
  • Treating comparison shopping as rejection
Better Approach

Acknowledge. Ask: "What will you be comparing?" Help them understand how to compare: scope, warranty, methodology, materials, experience.

Key Principle

The company that best explains the decision often earns the decision.

3

"It's too expensive."

Financial · Value Perception
▾
IF THIS…
"It's too expensive."

Almost never about the number itself.

THEN THAT…

Usually: "I wasn't expecting that" (framing failure), "I don't understand the value" (education gap), or "I'm afraid of making a mistake" (trust gap).

Great Reps Notice…
  • Objection came before full scope was presented
  • Customer didn't ask about what's included
  • They compared to a general contractor, not a specialist
  • They never asked about warranty or financing
Common Mistakes
  • Immediately offering the lowest price
  • Breaking down cost per pier without value
  • Comparing to other industries
  • Assuming they can't afford it
Better Approach

Acknowledge: "I understand. Most homeowners haven't purchased foundation repair before." Pause. Ask: "Was it higher than you expected, or is it more about deciding whether the repair is worth doing?"

Key Principle

Never lower price before raising understanding.

4

Customer introduces spouse late

Discovery · Hidden Stakeholders
▾
IF THIS…
"My husband/wife needs to see this."
THEN THAT…

Discovery failed to identify all decision-makers within the first 15 minutes. A 15-second question at the start prevents a 15-minute problem at the close.

Better Approach

During discovery ask: "Who will be involved in making this decision?" If absent at close, offer to prepare a summary or schedule a follow-up together.

Key Principle

Identify every decision-maker before you present, not after.

5

Customer stops asking questions

Trust · Disengagement Signal
▾
IF THIS…

Customer becomes quiet, stops asking clarifying questions, body language shifts.

THEN THAT…

They've shut down from overwhelm, lost trust, or mentally checked out. Silence is not agreement—it's withdrawal.

Better Approach

Stop. Pause. Ask: "I want to make sure I'm not losing you—is this making sense so far?" Reset around their priority, not your agenda.

Key Principle

When the customer stops talking, you've lost the appointment—not the sale.

6

"We can't afford it."

Financial · Empathy Required
▾
IF THIS…
"We just can't afford this right now."
THEN THAT…

They need help thinking through affordability without pressure. They may need to understand cost of inaction, or they may genuinely need to delay.

Common Mistakes
  • Minimizing: "It's really not that much"
  • Pushing financing before understanding
  • Using fear: "It'll cost more later"
  • Making them feel judged
Better Approach

Empathy first: "I understand. Protecting your home is important, but it has to fit your financial situation." Then explore gently. If they can't move forward, respect it.

Key Principle

Empathy creates more referrals than closing pressure ever will.

7

Customer asks about installation logistics

Buying Signal · They've decided "yes"
▾
IF THIS…

Customer asks: "How long will it take?" "Do we need to move furniture?" "When can you start?"

THEN THAT…

They've mentally moved past "whether" to "how". They're planning logistics. This is a strong buying signal.

Better Approach

Answer thoroughly, then transition: "Based on everything we've reviewed, here's what I recommend..."

Key Principle

When they ask "how," they've already decided "yes."

8

"We'll wait."

Timing · Urgency Unclear
▾
IF THIS…
"We're going to wait and see if it gets worse."
THEN THAT…

They haven't connected current symptoms to future risk. Foundation movement is progressive—it never resolves on its own.

Common Mistakes
  • Using fear: "Your house could collapse"
  • Creating false urgency: "Today only"
  • Accepting "wait" without exploring the real reason
Better Approach

Explore: "What would you like to wait for?" Then educate on progression without fear.

Key Principle

Educate on progression—never use fear to create urgency.

⚠ Common Mistakes

Every mistake below represents a pattern that average consultants repeat. Recognizing these in yourself is the fastest path to improvement.

1

Talking more than you listen

Process · Discovery
▾
The Mistake

Consultant dominates the conversation from arrival. By pricing, they have no idea what the customer cares about.

Why It Happens

Fear of silence. Insecurity. Belief that more information = more credibility.

The Fix

Follow the 70/30 rule: the customer should talk 70% of the time during discovery.

Key Principle

The consultant who asks the most questions controls the conversation.

2

Presenting before diagnosing

Process · Recommendation Discipline
▾
The Mistake

Recommending a solution before understanding motivation, timeline, decision process, or budget comfort.

Why It Happens

Overconfidence in technical findings. Skipping discovery because "the photos will convince them."

The Fix

Never recommend before understanding Problem, Impact, Priority, Decision Process, Budget Comfort, and Timeline.

Key Principle

Diagnose the person before diagnosing the home.

3

Using fear to create urgency

Communication · Trust
▾
The Mistake

"Your foundation is failing." "If you don't fix this now, it'll cost double." Fear-based selling destroys trust.

The Fix

Use data, not drama. "Based on these measurements, this movement is progressive." Let facts create urgency.

Key Principle

Data creates urgency. Fear destroys trust.

4

Discounting before understanding

Closing · Value
▾
The Mistake

Immediately offering lower price when customer hesitates at cost.

The Fix

Diagnose before solving. "Was it higher than you expected, or is it more about whether the repair is worth doing?"

Key Principle

Discounting before understanding destroys credibility.

5

Ignoring buying signals

Process · Awareness
▾
The Mistake

Customer asks about installation, timeline, or warranty—and consultant continues educating instead of closing.

The Fix

When they ask a logistics question, answer it, then transition. Buying signals are temporary.

Key Principle

When they ask "how," they've already decided "yes."

6

Being afraid of objections

Mindset · Confidence
▾
The Mistake

Avoiding presentation because they fear the customer will object. Under-educating, skipping recommendation, no clear next step.

The Fix

Reframe objections as questions. Every objection is an unanswered question. The more you handle, the more confident you become.

Key Principle

Objections aren't rejection—they're engagement.

7

Failing to identify decision-makers early

Process · Stakeholder Mapping
▾
The Mistake

90 minutes presenting to one person, only to discover at closing that a spouse needs to approve.

The Fix

Ask within the first 15 minutes: "Who will be involved in making this decision?"

Key Principle

Identify every decision-maker before you present.

8

Competing on price instead of value

Communication · Positioning
▾
The Mistake

Leading with price, competing against cheaper bids, positioning Baird as a commodity.

The Fix

Ask: "What will you be comparing?" Then educate on how to evaluate scope, warranty, engineering, methodology.

Key Principle

The company that best explains the decision often earns the decision.

9

Not narrating the inspection

Process · Transparency
▾
The Mistake

Disappearing for 45 minutes. Homeowner left alone with their imagination.

The Fix

Narrate as you go: "I'm checking whether these cracks line up with the elevation changes outside."

Key Principle

Transparency during inspection builds more trust than any presentation after it.

10

Not following up after objections

Mindset · Consistency
▾
The Mistake

"Call us when you're ready"—then never following up. Competitor who follows up wins.

The Fix

Follow up within 24 hours with a value-add touchpoint. Then every 3-5 days. Persistence demonstrates professionalism.

Key Principle

The fortune is in the follow-up.

★ Great Reps Recognize

Average consultants react. Great consultants recognize what customers are signaling before they say it.

1

Questions shift from "what" to "how"

Buying Signal · Commitment
▾
Great Reps Recognize…
  • Conversation shifted from education to logistics
  • Customer is visualizing the work happening
  • They're asking practical, not skeptical, questions
  • They've stopped pushing back and started planning
What Great Reps Do

Answer thoroughly, then transition to the close. Match their energy—they're in implementation mode.

Key Principle

Match the customer's phase—if they're planning, you should be too.

2

Customer stops making eye contact

Resistance Signal · Disengagement
▾
Great Reps Recognize…
  • The shift happened at a specific moment
  • They were engaged earlier but something changed
  • Questions became generic
  • Body language closed
What Great Reps Do

Stop presenting. Pause. Reset around their priority. "What's most important to you right now?"

Key Principle

When engagement drops, stop educating and start listening.

3

Customer says "that makes sense"

Buying Signal · Trust Building
▾
Great Reps Recognize…
  • This is the foundation of the entire sale
  • Confidence is being created through understanding
  • The customer is validating your expertise
  • The trust equation is working
What Great Reps Do

Reinforce without overselling. Don't over-explain. Continue the inspection. Keep momentum.

Key Principle

The moment they believe you is worth more than any close technique.

4

Late-stage objections appear

Trust Gift · Hidden Concerns
▾
Great Reps Recognize…
  • They trust you enough to be honest
  • These concerns were present from the beginning
  • Addressing them creates stronger commitment
What Great Reps Do

Thank them. Address it thoroughly. Confirm: "Does that fully address your concern?"

Key Principle

Late objections are trust gifts—welcome them.

5

Customer shares personal stories

Trust Signal · Vulnerability
▾
Great Reps Recognize…
  • Trust established at a personal level
  • They're giving you information you didn't ask for
  • They may be revealing their real motivation
What Great Reps Do

Listen fully. Acknowledge. Connect your recommendation to what matters to them personally.

Key Principle

When they share their story, they've given you the key to the sale.

6

Customer asks about warranty

Buying Signal · Long-term Thinking
▾
Great Reps Recognize…
  • They're thinking years ahead—commitment signal
  • They want assurance the investment holds up
  • Warranty questions often precede close questions
What Great Reps Do

Answer thoroughly and confidently. Connect it back to their motivation.

Key Principle

Warranty questions are buying signals—answer with confidence.

7

Silence after pricing

Processing · Not Rejection
▾
Great Reps Recognize…
  • Silence is processing, not rejection
  • They may be doing mental math
  • Rushing to fill the silence destroys the moment
What Great Reps Do

Wait. Count to ten. Then gently: "What questions does this bring up?"

Key Principle

Silence after pricing is sacred. Respect it.

8

Customer invites you to sit down

Trust Signal · Acceptance
▾
Great Reps Recognize…
  • They've accepted you as a guest, not a salesperson
  • This is a significant trust milestone
  • Opportunity for deeper discovery
What Great Reps Do

Accept graciously. Deepen discovery. The kitchen table is where relationships are built.

Key Principle

When they invite you in, honor that by being genuine.

📖 Field Guide

Quick reference for common field situations.

D

Door Sticking

Common Symptom
▾
Possible Causes
  • Differential settlement shifting the frame
  • Moisture causing wood swelling
  • Original installation tolerance exceeded
Questions To Ask
  • "When did you first notice it sticking?"
  • "Is it getting worse over time?"
  • "Interior or exterior doors?"
Customer Explanation

"When doors start sticking, it usually means the foundation has shifted slightly. It's one of the most common early warning signs—and it's progressive."

W

Wall Cracks

Structural Indicator
▾
Possible Causes
  • Settlement pattern (diagonal, stair-step, horizontal)
  • Shrinkage cracks (cosmetic)
  • Soil expansion/contraction cycle
Questions To Ask
  • "Is this crack new or has it been here a while?"
  • "Has it gotten wider?"
  • "Can you fit a pencil in it?"
Customer Explanation

"The pattern tells me what's happening underneath. A diagonal crack from a window corner usually means settlement. Let me show you what I'm seeing."

R

Customer Has YouTube Research

DIY Researcher
▾
Great Reps Handle This By…
  • Respecting their research: "That's great you've looked into this"
  • Never arguing with what they've seen online
  • Inviting discussion: "What did you find most helpful?"
  • Connecting general info to their specific findings
Key Principle

A customer who researches is a customer who cares.

C

Comparing to a General Contractor

Specialist vs. Generalist
▾
Great Reps Handle This By…
  • Never disparaging the contractor
  • Educating on the difference: engineering, warranty, methodology
  • Asking: "What warranty did they offer?"
Key Principle

Differentiate through education, not criticism.

◆ Manager Coaching

Coaching develops judgment, not scripts. The best managers teach consultants to diagnose situations.

1

The Performance Diagnostic Flow

Diagnose the root cause
▾
Diagnose This Sequence
  • Step 1: Enough appointments? No → Activity coaching
  • Step 2: Inspection quality strong? No → Technical training
  • Step 3: Discovery complete? No → Questioning coaching
  • Step 4: Presentation effective? No → Communication coaching
  • Step 5: Objections managed? No → Objection practice
  • Step 6: Closing confidence? No → Closing coaching
Questions To Ask

"Walk me through your last three appointments. At what point did each customer disengage? If you could restart one five-minute segment, which would it be?"

2

The 5-Question Ride-Along Debrief

Self-awareness before coaching
▾
Ask These 5 Questions (In Order)
  1. "What went well today that you'd repeat?"
  2. "Where did you feel the customer's confidence increase?"
  3. "Where did the conversation become more difficult?"
  4. "If you could restart one five-minute segment, which would it be?"
  5. "What's one behavior you'll improve before your next appointment?"
Key Principle

Self-assessment develops judgment. Lecturing develops dependence.

3

Questions That Develop Judgment

Instead of telling, ask
▾
Don't Ask

"What should you have said?" "Why didn't you close?"

Ask Instead
  • "What confidence was missing?"
  • "When did the conversation change?"
  • "What evidence did you present?"
  • "What question should you have asked instead?"
Key Principle

The best managers create consultants who can diagnose and adapt.
